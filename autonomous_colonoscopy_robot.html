Why this project?

Colonoscopy is clinically important but physically demanding for operators. Traditional procedures require coordinated pushing, pulling, rotation, and bending of a flexible colonoscope. This project targets autonomous colonoscope navigation by combining a robotic actuation platform with learning-based visual control.

Clinical motivation

Reducing operator fatigue and improving procedure consistency can support safer and more efficient colorectal cancer screening.

Robotic platform

The 3-RCS performs left-right steering, up-down steering, and forward-backward insertion while keeping the flexible colonoscope's motion range.

Learning-based autonomy

Deep models predict steering points, collision risk, steering signals, and feeding velocity directly from colonoscopic images.

Robotic colonoscopy system

The proposed 3-RCS uses a feeding mechanism with rollers for smooth insertion and retraction, and a tendon-driven steering mechanism actuated by two motors. The system was validated through hysteresis experiments for steering and insertion accuracy before autonomous control experiments.

Three learning-based methods

The study compares three autonomous control strategies. The final end-to-end method predicts all required control signals using one model, reducing complexity while improving intubation time, success rate, and stability.

METHOD A

Model 1 + IBVS

Model 1 predicts a steering point and collision probability from the colonoscopic image. IBVS then generates steering signals.

METHOD B

Model 1 + Model 2

Model 2 predicts steering signals from steering points, removing real-time pulley-angle feedback used in the IBVS pipeline.

METHOD C

End-to-End model

A single model predicts steering signals, collision probability, and feeding speed directly from colonoscopic images.

Dataset generation

Dataset 1 was generated by manually operating the robotic colonoscopy system in a colonoscopy training simulator. A total of 27 experiments produced 52,771 images. After training Model 1, additional autonomous trials were used to create Dataset 2 for steering-signal prediction and Dataset 3 for end-to-end control signal prediction.

Labels and signals

Images were paired with steering point coordinates, wall/non-wall collision labels, steering velocities, collision probability, and feeding speed depending on the dataset.

Model architecture and prediction examples

Model 1 and Model E2E use pretrained CNN backbones including ResNet-18, ResNet-34, MobileNetV2, and DenseNet-121. Model 2 uses a 1D-CNN-LSTM architecture to predict steering signals from steering-point sequences.

Key quantitative results

Method C achieved the best autonomous performance, reducing mean cecal intubation time while improving the cecal intubation rate compared with Methods A and B.

Main contributions

1. 3-RCS robotic platform

Designed a 3-DOF robotic colonoscopy system for insertion/retraction and two-axis steering while remaining compatible with conventional flexible colonoscopes.

2. Autonomous image-based control

Developed deep learning models that infer steering direction and collision probability from colonoscopic images.

3. End-to-end signal prediction

Introduced a single-model method that outputs steering signals, collision probability, and feeding speed for autonomous operation.

4. Experimental validation

Evaluated the proposed methods in a colonoscopy training simulator and compared them with novice human operators.
